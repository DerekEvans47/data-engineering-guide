8.2

Clustering & Unsupervised Learning

What machine learning looks like when there's no right answer to learn from, the three algorithms that group similar things together in fundamentally different ways, and why a clustering result is a starting hypothesis — not a discovered truth

  2. Describe how K-means partitions data around K centroids, why K must be chosen in advance, and why feature scaling is required before running it

8.2.1

Unsupervised Learning: No Labels, Just Structure

Every algorithm in section 8.1 needed a known, correct answer to learn from — a label. Unsupervised learning removes that requirement entirely. There is no "right answer" column in the training data at all. Instead of learning a mapping from features to a known label, an unsupervised algorithm looks for structure that's already latent in the data itself — and the most common form that structure takes is clustering: grouping similar rows together based on how close their feature values are to each other, with no prior knowledge of what the groups should be or how many there should be.

This matters in practice for problems where you genuinely don't have labels to begin with, or where discovering the groups is the point. A marketing team doesn't start with a pre-existing "customer segment" column in their CRM (Customer Relationship Management system) — clustering is how that column gets created in the first place, by finding groups of customers with similar purchasing behavior. A security team monitoring server logs doesn't have a label for "this is a coordinated attack pattern" until clustering surfaces a dense group of unusual requests that looks nothing like normal traffic. In both cases, the algorithm isn't predicting a known outcome — it's proposing a way to organize the data that a human then has to interpret and validate.

Supervised learning answers a question you already know how to phrase: "will this customer churn?" Unsupervised learning answers a question you didn't know you needed to ask yet: "are there natural groups of customers behaving differently from each other, and if so, what are they?"

The three algorithms in this section — K-means, hierarchical clustering, and DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) — all solve the clustering problem, but they disagree fundamentally on what makes a group of points "a cluster" in the first place. That disagreement is not a minor technical detail; it's the entire reason all three algorithms still exist side by side rather than one having simply won.

8.2.2

K-Means: Partitioning Around K Centers

K-means is the most widely used clustering algorithm, largely because it's fast, simple to reason about, and scales well to large datasets. The "K" is a number you choose before running the algorithm at all — the number of clusters you want it to find. The algorithm then repeats a simple two-step loop until it stabilizes:

1. Assign: place each data point into whichever cluster has the nearest centroid — a centroid being simply the current center point (the mean position) of a cluster. 2. Update: recompute each centroid as the actual mean of all the points now assigned to it, which moves the centroid to sit at the true center of its newly assigned group. These two steps repeat — reassign points to the now-updated centroids, recompute the centroids again — until the centroids stop moving meaningfully between iterations, at which point the algorithm has converged.

Figure 8.3K-means converges by repeatedly assigning points to their nearest centroid and then recomputing each centroid as the mean of its assigned points. The loop ends once an iteration produces no meaningful centroid movement. The number of iterations needed varies, but convergence on well-separated data is typically fast.

K-means' speed and simplicity come with real assumptions that don't always hold. It implicitly assumes clusters are roughly spherical (round, not elongated or irregularly shaped) and similarly sized, because "nearest centroid by straight-line distance" is a geometric rule that favors round blobs. It's also sensitive to the random starting positions of the initial centroids — a poor random start can converge to a worse final result, which is why production implementations typically run the algorithm multiple times with different random starts and keep the best outcome. And because the algorithm is entirely distance-based, every feature must be put on a comparable numeric scale first — a detail important enough to get its own deep dive below.

+
Go DeeperChoosing K: the elbow method and silhouette score

K-means requires you to specify the number of clusters before it runs — but in most real problems, you don't actually know the "right" number of customer segments or log patterns in advance. Two standard techniques help estimate a reasonable K, though neither produces a single objectively correct answer.

The elbow method: run K-means repeatedly for a range of K values (say, 2 through 10), and for each, record the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS) — the total squared distance from every point to its assigned centroid. WCSS always decreases as K increases (more clusters can always fit the data more tightly, up to the trivial extreme of one cluster per point), but the rate of improvement typically slows sharply at some point, forming a bend or "elbow" on the chart. That elbow is a reasonable candidate for K — beyond it, adding more clusters buys you very little additional tightness for a meaningfully more complex result.

Silhouette score: for each point, this measures how much closer it is, on average, to points in its own cluster compared to points in the nearest other cluster, producing a score from -1 (likely misclassified) to +1 (well-clustered). Averaging this across all points gives a single score per candidate K; the K with the highest average silhouette score is often preferred over the elbow method because it directly measures cluster separation rather than relying on a visually judged bend in a chart.

The honest caveat: both methods are heuristics, not proofs. Real business data frequently doesn't have a clean elbow or an unambiguous best silhouette score — in practice, the chosen K is also informed by how many segments the business can realistically act on. A marketing team that can only design five distinct campaigns has little use for a statistically "optimal" K of 14.

🏢Field Note
A Retail Company  ·  Five Segments, One Useful Story

A retail company's data science team ran K-means on customer purchase history — recency, frequency, and average order value, the classic "RFM" feature set — and the silhouette score pointed to K=7 as statistically optimal. The marketing team, looking at the resulting seven segments, could only articulate a distinct campaign strategy for about four of them; the other three looked different in the numbers but the marketers couldn't say what, in practice, they would do differently for each.

Rather than treating the statistically optimal K as final, the data team re-ran the clustering at K=5 and worked with marketing to manually examine and name each resulting group: "Champions" (high frequency, high spend), "At-Risk High-Value" (high spend historically, declining recency), "New & Promising," "Price-Sensitive Regulars," and "Dormant." Every one of the five had both a clean numeric signature and a specific, actionable campaign attached to it. The slightly "worse" K, by the silhouette metric, was the genuinely useful one.

Eighteen months later, the five-segment model was still in active use, regularly refreshed with new data, while a separate K=7 attempt commissioned by a different team had been quietly abandoned after nobody could explain three of its clusters to the campaign planning meeting.

The Lesson

A clustering metric tells you how mathematically separated your groups are. It says nothing about whether a human being can act on them. The right K is the largest number of clusters the business can actually tell a different story about — not necessarily the number with the best silhouette score.

8.2.3

Hierarchical Clustering

Hierarchical clustering takes a fundamentally different approach: instead of committing to a fixed number of clusters upfront, it builds an entire nested tree of groupings, called a dendrogram, and lets you decide afterward how many clusters to extract by choosing where to "cut" the tree. The most common variant, agglomerative clustering, builds the tree bottom-up: it starts by treating every single data point as its own tiny cluster, then repeatedly merges the two closest clusters into one, continuing until everything has been merged into a single root cluster containing the entire dataset.

"Closest" between two clusters (rather than two individual points) needs its own definition, called the linkage method. Common choices include single linkage (distance between the closest pair of points across the two clusters), complete linkage (distance between the farthest pair), average linkage (the mean of all pairwise distances), and Ward's method (the merge that increases total within-cluster variance the least). The choice of linkage materially changes the resulting tree shape, and switching linkage methods on the same data can produce noticeably different dendrograms — there's no universally correct choice, only one better suited to the specific shape of clusters you expect.

The dendrogram itself is the algorithm's signature advantage: it's a single, visual artifact that shows every possible number of clusters simultaneously, as a height at which you choose to cut the tree. Cutting near the top yields very few, very broad clusters; cutting lower yields more, finer-grained ones — and unlike K-means, you can make that call by inspecting the actual structure of the data rather than guessing K before running anything. The cost is computational: comparing every pair of clusters at every merge step scales quadratically or worse with the number of data points, which makes hierarchical clustering impractical on datasets much larger than a few tens of thousands of rows — exactly the scale where K-means and DBSCAN remain comfortable.

+
Go DeeperScaling matters: why distance-based clustering needs standardized features

K-means, hierarchical clustering, and DBSCAN are all, fundamentally, distance calculators — they group points based on how numerically close they are to each other. That makes feature scaling a non-optional pre-processing step, in a way it often isn't for tree-based models from section 8.1.

Concretely: imagine clustering customers using two features, "annual income" (ranging roughly from $20,000 to $200,000) and "number of logins per week" (ranging from 0 to 20). Because the income values are numerically thousands of times larger than the login values, a straight-line distance calculation will be almost entirely dominated by income differences — two customers who are wildly different in login behavior but similar in income will be judged "close" by the algorithm, regardless of how different their actual engagement pattern is. The login feature has effectively been erased from the clustering, not because it's unimportant, but purely because of its numeric scale.

The standard fix is to standardize every feature before clustering — typically by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, so every feature ends up on a comparable scale (centered at 0, with most values falling roughly between -3 and +3) regardless of its original units. This is a single line of code in any standard ML library, but skipping it is one of the most common, and most silently damaging, mistakes in practical clustering work — the algorithm runs without error and produces a result that looks plausible, but is dominated by whichever feature happened to have the largest raw numeric range.

Decision trees and random forests, by contrast, split on one feature threshold at a time rather than computing combined distances, which is exactly why they don't require this step — a detail worth being able to articulate clearly if asked to compare preprocessing requirements across algorithm families.

8.2.4

DBSCAN: Clustering by Density, Not Distance to a Center

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) abandons the idea of a cluster having a "center" at all. Instead, it defines a cluster as a region where data points are packed densely together, separated from other dense regions by areas of relative emptiness — much closer to how a human eye intuitively spots groups in a scatter plot than either K-means' centroids or hierarchical clustering's pairwise merges.

DBSCAN takes two parameters: epsilon (ε), a distance defining how close two points need to be to count as neighbors, and minPts, the minimum number of neighbors a point needs within that distance to be considered part of a dense region (a "core point"). The algorithm expands clusters outward from core points by repeatedly absorbing their neighbors, and any point that never ends up within reach of a dense region — too far from everything else to belong anywhere — is explicitly labeled noise rather than being forced into the nearest cluster regardless of fit.

Figure 8.4The same elongated, non-round cluster shape, handled two ways. K-means (left) can only think in terms of distance to a centroid, so it cuts the one true cluster into two arbitrary halves along a straight boundary. DBSCAN (right) follows the actual density-connected shape as a single cluster, and the scattered red points — too far from any dense region — are correctly labeled noise rather than forced into a cluster they don't belong to.

This is DBSCAN's signature strength: it makes no assumption that clusters are round or similarly sized, so it correctly identifies clusters of essentially any shape, as long as the points within each one are densely connected. It also naturally produces a built-in outlier detector for free — any point flagged as noise is, by definition, an anomaly relative to every dense region in the dataset, which is exactly why DBSCAN shows up frequently in fraud detection and sensor anomaly use cases, not just conventional segmentation.

The tradeoff is sensitivity to its two parameters. Too small an epsilon, and almost every point gets flagged as noise because nothing looks dense enough; too large, and distinct clusters merge into one because the search radius now bridges the gap between them. DBSCAN also struggles when the dataset genuinely contains clusters of meaningfully different densities — a single epsilon value that works well for a tight, dense cluster may be far too small to detect a second, more spread-out one in the same dataset.

+
Go DeeperMatching the algorithm to the use case: segmentation, anomaly detection, and log clustering

The three algorithms in this section are not interchangeable defaults — the right one depends heavily on what kind of clustering problem you actually have.

Customer segmentation (marketing, RFM analysis) typically has a moderate number of customers, a business need for a specific, fixed number of actionable segments, and no strong expectation of bizarrely shaped groups. K-means is usually the practical default here: it's fast, scales to large customer bases, and the requirement to pick K is actually a feature, not a bug, since the business usually wants a specific, bounded number of segments to design campaigns around.

Anomaly and fraud detection needs exactly the opposite property: most data is "normal," a small fraction is anomalous, and you generally have no idea in advance how many anomaly patterns exist or what shape they take. DBSCAN fits naturally — its noise classification is the anomaly detector, with no separate step required, and it makes no assumption about how many "types" of anomaly exist.

Log clustering and taxonomy discovery (grouping similar error messages, support tickets, or document types when you don't yet know the right categories) often benefits from hierarchical clustering specifically because the dendrogram itself becomes a usable artifact — engineers can visually inspect the tree, decide that some sub-branches should be merged or split further, and use the hierarchy directly as a draft taxonomy, rather than just consuming a flat list of cluster assignments.

A practical pattern worth knowing for interviews: it's common to run more than one of these as a first-pass exploration — K-means for a quick, fast read on roughly how many natural groups exist, then DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering for a more careful pass once the shape and density of the data is better understood.

8.2.5

When Clusters Are (and Aren't) Meaningful

There is an uncomfortable fact about every clustering algorithm covered in this section: each one will produce groups whether or not any real structure actually exists in the data. Feed K-means pure random noise and ask it for three clusters, and it will confidently hand back three clusters — boundaries drawn somewhere, centroids placed somewhere, with no error message telling you the result is meaningless. Unlike supervised learning, where a held-out test set gives you an objective accuracy number, there is no equivalent ground truth to check a clustering result against, because there was never a label to begin with.

This makes human validation a mandatory step, not an optional nice-to-have. Beyond the elbow method and silhouette score covered earlier — which measure mathematical separation, not real-world relevance — the practical test is whether someone with domain expertise can look at each cluster and articulate a specific, coherent story about who or what is in it, and ideally a different action for each one. The retail field note above is the positive version of this test passing; a clustering result where every group's story is "it's complicated" or "they're all kind of similar to each other" is a signal to revisit feature selection, try a different K, or reconsider whether clustering was the right tool for the problem at all.

🏢Field Note
A SaaS Security Team  ·  The Cluster That Was Just Time Zones

A SaaS company's security team ran DBSCAN on login event data — IP geolocation distance, time-of-day, and login frequency — looking for coordinated account takeover patterns. The algorithm surfaced a clean, dense cluster of around 200 accounts with unusual late-night login times, distinct from the bulk of normal daytime traffic. Initial excitement: this looked exactly like the kind of anomalous pattern they were hunting for.

Before escalating to a security incident, an analyst cross-referenced the flagged accounts against customer account metadata and found the explanation in about twenty minutes: nearly all 200 accounts belonged to customers in a specific overseas region whose business hours, relative to the company's UTC-based "time of day" feature, simply landed in what looked like "late night" from the feature's perspective. The cluster was real — DBSCAN had correctly found a dense, distinct group — but the story behind it was a time zone artifact, not an attack.

The team's fix wasn't to distrust DBSCAN; the density detection had worked exactly as designed. It was to engineer a better feature: time-of-day relative to each account's own registered time zone, rather than a single global UTC clock. Re-run on the corrected feature, the false cluster disappeared entirely, and a much smaller, genuinely suspicious cluster of accounts with truly anomalous behavior — relative to their own normal hours — became visible instead.

The Lesson

A statistically real, dense, well-separated cluster can still be a feature engineering artifact rather than a meaningful pattern. Validating a cluster's story against independent business context — not just admiring how clean it looks mathematically — is what catches this before it becomes a false security alert, a wasted marketing campaign, or worse.

✓ Section Summary

Unsupervised learning finds structure in data with no known label to learn from; clustering is its most common application. K-means partitions data around K centroids chosen in advance, iterating between assignment and recomputation until convergence — fast and scalable, but assumes round, similarly sized clusters and requires standardized features. Hierarchical clustering builds a full dendrogram of nested groupings with no need to choose K upfront, at the cost of poor scalability to large datasets. DBSCAN clusters by density rather than distance to a center, naturally handling irregular shapes and flagging sparse points as noise, making it a strong fit for anomaly detection. No clustering algorithm can confirm its own output is meaningful — random data still produces clusters — so validating that a human can tell a coherent, actionable story about each group is a required step, not an afterthought.

A

Question 3 of 5

A dataset contains one true cluster shaped like a thin, curved crescent. K-means is run on it with K=2. What is the most likely outcome, and why?

A
K-means will correctly identify the crescent as a single cluster and place one extra, empty cluster elsewhere
B
K-means will likely split the single crescent-shaped cluster into two arbitrary halves along a straight boundary, because it can only group points by distance to a centroid and has no concept of the cluster's actual curved shape
C
K-means will automatically detect the non-round shape and switch to a density-based approach internally
D
K-means will fail to converge and return no result for non-round cluster shapes

B is correct. This is precisely the scenario shown in Figure 8.4. K-means' entire notion of a cluster is "points closer to centroid A than to centroid B," which is a fundamentally round-shaped assumption. A thin, curved cluster gets sliced by whatever straight boundary minimizes distance to two centroids, producing two clusters that don't correspond to any real structure. DBSCAN, which groups by density along the actual shape of the data rather than distance to a center, handles this case correctly.

Question 4 of 5

A fraud analytics team doesn't know in advance how many distinct fraud patterns exist in their transaction data, and expects most transactions to be normal with only a small, scattered fraction being anomalous. Which clustering algorithm is the most natural fit, and why?

A
K-means, because it is the fastest algorithm and speed matters most for fraud detection
B
Hierarchical clustering, because the dendrogram will clearly separate fraud from non-fraud at the top level of the tree
C
DBSCAN, because it requires no upfront assumption about the number of clusters, and its noise classification directly serves as an anomaly detector — exactly matching a problem where most data is normal and anomalies are sparse and not pre-counted
D
Any of the three algorithms would perform identically on this type of problem

C is correct. DBSCAN's defining property — points too sparse to belong to any dense region are explicitly labeled noise rather than forced into a cluster — is a direct match for fraud and anomaly detection, where the number of "normal" and "anomalous" pattern types is unknown in advance. K-means would force every fraudulent transaction into one of K predetermined clusters regardless of how well it actually fits, and hierarchical clustering doesn't have a notion of "noise" either — every point still ends up merged into the tree somewhere.

Question 5 of 5

A team runs K-means on random, structureless data and it confidently returns four distinct clusters with specific boundaries. What does this demonstrate, and what is the correct response?

A
This is a bug — a properly implemented clustering algorithm should return an error or no clusters when given random data
B
Clustering algorithms will always produce some grouping of the data, regardless of whether any real structure exists — there is no built-in check against meaninglessness. The correct response is to validate any clustering result against independent domain knowledge or business context before acting on it, not to trust the algorithm's output at face value
C
This proves K=4 was the correct number of clusters, since the algorithm converged successfully
D
This only happens with K-means specifically; DBSCAN and hierarchical clustering are immune to this issue

B is correct. Unlike supervised learning, clustering has no held-out test set or accuracy metric to check the result against ground truth, because there was never a label to begin with. Every clustering algorithm covered in this section — not just K-means — will produce some output even on data with no real structure (the SaaS security field note shows DBSCAN producing a "real" but meaningless cluster driven by a time zone artifact). Treating a clustering result as a starting hypothesis to validate with domain context, rather than a discovered fact, is the only defense against acting on a meaningless grouping.

No Label, No Ground Truth

Clustering has no accuracy metric to check against, because there's no known correct answer. Every algorithm will produce groups even on random data — validation against domain context is mandatory, not optional.

Pick the Algorithm by the Question

Fixed number of segments needed → K-means. Want a visual taxonomy to explore → hierarchical. Don't know how many groups exist and need outliers flagged → DBSCAN.

Always Scale First

K-means, hierarchical clustering, and DBSCAN are all distance-based. A feature with a larger numeric range will silently dominate the result unless every feature is standardized first.

Interview Insight

"How would you choose K for K-means?" is a common screening question. Mentioning the elbow method and silhouette score shows technical knowledge; adding that the final choice should also be constrained by what the business can actually act on is the senior-level answer.
