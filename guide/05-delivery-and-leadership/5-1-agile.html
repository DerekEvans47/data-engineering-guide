5.1

Agile for Data Teams

Why standard Scrum breaks for data work, what to use instead, and how to run a data team that delivers consistently rather than heroically

✓

Expected Learning Outcomes

  1. Explain the three specific ways standard Scrum fails for data engineering work and why those failures are structural, not team-specific
  2. Design a Kanban workflow appropriate for a data engineering team, including WIP (Work in Progress) limits and column definitions
  3. Describe the adaptations required to make Scrum workable for data — what to keep, what to drop, and what to modify
  4. Explain the Shape Up methodology and why its appetite-based approach fits data projects better than story-point estimation

5.1.1

Why Delivery Methodology Belongs in a Data Engineering Guide

The first four parts of this guide covered the technical stack: ingestion, storage, transformation, modeling, and visualization. You could master every tool in that stack and still fail at your job if you cannot deliver work predictably, manage stakeholder expectations under uncertainty, and run a team that doesn't burn out firefighting production incidents from the night before a sprint review.

A senior data engineering role is a delivery leadership role. You are responsible not just for the quality of what you build, but for the cadence at which you build it, the clarity with which you communicate progress and blockers, and the process your team follows when the unexpected happens — which in data engineering, is constantly. "Technical depth plus delivery craft" is the combination that distinguishes a senior practitioner from a strong individual contributor.

This section is about process, not tools. It covers the three delivery methodologies most relevant to data teams — Scrum, Kanban, and Shape Up — with honest assessments of where each fits and where each fails. It then covers the ceremonies, artefacts, and habits that improve delivery regardless of which methodology your organization has chosen.

Data engineering is a discipline where the work is inherently exploratory, the dependencies are often external, and the "definition of done" requires a business stakeholder's agreement, not just a passing test suite. A methodology designed for none of those conditions will fail silently until it fails catastrophically.
5.1.2

Where Standard Scrum Breaks for Data

Scrum was designed for software product teams building features with relatively predictable engineering complexity. The framework assumes: you can estimate work in advance with reasonable accuracy, you know what "done" looks like before you start, and the blockers you face are mostly internal to the team. Data engineering violates all three assumptions regularly.

Velocity Cannot Be Predicted for Exploratory Work

Scrum measures team capacity through velocity — the number of story points completed per sprint, used to plan future sprints. Velocity planning requires that the team's work be sufficiently similar in nature from sprint to sprint that past performance predicts future capacity. Data engineering includes work that fits this model (building a well-defined pipeline from a known schema to a known destination) and work that absolutely does not (investigating why three data sources produce inconsistent customer counts, profiling a source system you've never seen before, debugging a PySpark job that runs correctly on a 1% sample and fails on full data).

When exploratory work lands in a Scrum sprint, it either blows the velocity and the sprint fails, or the engineer pre-empts the failure by inflating their estimate — which makes velocity data meaningless as a planning tool. Neither outcome is good. Teams that force data exploration into story-point estimates are either perpetually "behind" or perpetually dishonest about capacity.

The Definition of Done Is Rarely Binary

In software engineering, a feature is done when it passes tests, is reviewed, and is deployed. Done is binary. In data engineering, "done" often means: the pipeline runs, the data looks correct to us, but we haven't confirmed it with the Finance team, who won't be available to validate until next Tuesday. Is it done? Most Scrum definitions would say no — but if you block completion on stakeholder validation for every data deliverable, your sprint velocity trends toward zero.

Data quality exists on a spectrum. A pipeline that processes 99.8% of records correctly might be deployable for operational use while the 0.2% exception rate is under investigation — or it might be completely unacceptable depending on what those records represent. "Done enough to deploy" and "done by any definition" are genuinely different states for data work, and Scrum's binary Done/Not Done structure doesn't accommodate the distinction.

Data Work Is Perpetually Blocked by External Dependencies

A data engineering team cannot fully control its own throughput. A sprint item that requires a new database account in the source system is blocked until IT provisions it. A model validation requires a business analyst's time, which isn't in your sprint plan. An upstream schema change breaks three pipelines on day four of the sprint. None of these blockers are failures of the team — they are structural properties of data work, where every input comes from someone else's system and every output needs someone else's sign-off.

Scrum's daily stand-up structure exposes blockers but provides no mechanism for managing them. A team running 40% blocked velocity over three sprints will trigger a Scrum Master retrospective asking "how do we remove blockers?" — when the honest answer is "we can't, they're structural." The better question is: "how do we design our workflow so that blocked items don't halt all other progress?"

🏢Field Note
A Healthcare Organization  ·  The Sprint Failure Cascade

A healthcare data engineering team adopted Scrum for a data warehouse migration project. Sprint 1 included provisioning access to seven source systems. Access to two was delayed by the security team, leaving three dependent pipeline tasks blocked. The sprint ended at 60% velocity. Sprint 2 carried forward the blocked items and planned new work on top. Mid-sprint, a schema change in the EHR (Electronic Health Record) system broke two pipelines. Sprint 2 ended at 55% velocity. By Sprint 4, the team's sprint board had more items carried forward from previous sprints than new work — a backlog growing faster than throughput.

The retrospective produced action items about "improving communication with IT" and "better sprint planning." Neither addressed the structural problem: the work required external provisioning and upstream system stability that the team could not control. The project moved to a Kanban approach in month three. The same people, same tools, same work — but with Kanban's explicit handling of blocked items (visible in a "Blocked" swimlane with timestamps), the team could show exactly which work was progressing and which was pending external parties. Stakeholder reporting became honest rather than optimistic.

The Lesson

Sprint failure cascades in data teams are almost never caused by poor engineering. They are caused by forcing a methodology designed for internally-unblocked work onto a discipline where external dependencies are the norm. The fix is methodological, not motivational.

5.1.3

Kanban for Data Engineering

Kanban is a flow-based methodology with no sprints, no velocity targets, and no commitment to completing a fixed set of items in a fixed time period. Work items enter a queue and flow through defined stages to completion. The mechanism that makes Kanban work is the WIP limit — a cap on the number of items allowed in any given stage simultaneously. When a stage hits its WIP limit, no new work can enter until existing work exits. This forces the team to finish things rather than start things.

For data engineering, Kanban's core advantages are structural: blocked items are visible without failing a sprint, exploratory work can sit in the "In Progress" column for as long as it takes without distorting velocity metrics, and new urgent work (a production pipeline failure, a last-minute reporting request) can enter the queue without displacing committed sprint items.

Designing a Data Engineering Kanban Board

A generic Kanban board (To Do → In Progress → Done) is too coarse for data engineering work. The transitions between stages are where work gets lost or stuck. A more useful column structure reflects the actual stages data work moves through, with WIP limits that account for team size and typical work types:

Figure 5.1A data engineering Kanban board with five stages and explicit WIP limits. The Exploration column (WIP: 2) contains open-ended investigation work that would be impossible to sprint-size — it sits here until the team understands the problem well enough to move it to development. The blocked card in Development is visible to stakeholders with a timestamp, showing external dependency without halting the rest of the work. WIP limits on Development (3) and Review (2) prevent work from piling up in any single stage and create natural pull pressure to clear items before starting new ones.

WIP Limits in Practice

The WIP limit is the mechanism that makes Kanban work. Without it, Kanban is just a list of tasks in columns. A WIP limit of 3 on the Development column means that when three items are in development, no new item can enter until one exits. This forces engineers to finish things — to review, unblock, or accelerate existing work — rather than starting new work when blocked. It also surfaces bottlenecks: if Review is perpetually at its WIP limit, the bottleneck is not development, it's review capacity.

Go DeeperWriting a Definition of Done for data engineering work

The Definition of Done (DoD) is the shared agreement of what "complete" means for a work item. In software engineering, it's usually: code reviewed, tests passing, deployed. For data engineering work, the DoD must be more explicit because data quality is a spectrum and stakeholder validation is often required.

A usable data engineering DoD template:

For a new pipeline: Source data profiled and documented. Pipeline runs end-to-end in dev environment. Row counts reconciled against source (within agreed tolerance). dbt tests passing (not_null, unique, accepted_values, referential integrity). Code reviewed by one peer. PR merged and deployed to production. Deployment run validated in production. Business owner has signed off on sample output.

For a new data model: Grain documented. Surrogate keys generated. SCD type specified for each dimension. dbt tests cover primary key, foreign keys, and key measures. Documentation written in schema.yml. Business stakeholder has validated at least one report built on the model against source figures.

For a data quality investigation: Root cause identified and documented. Ticket raised with upstream team if external fix required. Impact assessed (how many records, which downstream reports affected). Short-term fix deployed if one exists. Monitoring added to detect recurrence. Summary communicated to stakeholders.

The key principle: business stakeholder validation should be in the DoD, not treated as post-completion sign-off. If a model is "done" but not validated, it is not done — it is done-ish, which means it will be un-done when someone finds a discrepancy. Move the validation step forward, not backward.

5.1.4

Adapted Scrum

Many data teams operate in organizations that have standardized on Scrum across all engineering functions. Abandoning it entirely is not an option. The right response is not compliance theater — pretending the methodology fits when it doesn't — but thoughtful adaptation that preserves the value Scrum delivers (cadence, visibility, retrospective learning) while addressing the specific failure modes for data work.

What to Keep

The sprint cadence: Two-week sprints create a forcing function for prioritization and a heartbeat for stakeholder communication. Knowing there is a demo every two weeks focuses both the team and the stakeholders. Keep the cadence even if you drop velocity tracking.

Retrospectives: The most valuable Scrum ceremony for data teams. Unlike sprint reviews, which are about output, retrospectives are about process — what slowed us down, what we'd do differently, what one thing would most improve the next sprint. A 45-minute retrospective every two weeks, done well, compounds in value over months.

The data demo: Rather than a traditional sprint review where the team demos software features, a data sprint demo shows: a new Power BI report built on the data, a test suite passing on a new pipeline, a data quality dashboard showing improved coverage. Demonstrating working data — not just working code — keeps stakeholders connected to what the team is actually delivering.

What to Modify

Story points → rough sizing (S/M/L/XL): Story points create false precision. An "8-point story" implies a level of predictability that data engineering tasks rarely justify. Replace story points with rough sizing: Small (a few hours, well-understood), Medium (one to three days, mostly clear), Large (three to five days, some unknowns), XL (over a week, significant unknowns — should probably be broken down before committing). Use these sizes for capacity planning, not velocity tracking.

Sprint buffer: Reserve 15–20% of sprint capacity for unplanned work — production pipeline failures, urgent reporting requests, data quality investigations triggered by the business. If data engineers carry no buffer, unplanned work either blows sprint commitments or gets ignored until it becomes critical. A standing buffer normalizes this reality rather than treating every production incident as a sprint disruption.

Explicit blocked state: Add a "Blocked" column or swimlane to the sprint board. Every blocked item should display: what it's blocked on, who owns the blocker, and how long it has been blocked. This changes the conversation from "why isn't this done?" to "the item has been pending IT provisioning for 6 days — can we escalate?"

5.1.5

Shape Up — The Alternative Worth Knowing

Shape Up is a product development methodology developed by Basecamp (the company behind the project management tool), described in their freely available book of the same name. It is not widely used outside software product companies — but its core ideas are an exceptionally good fit for data engineering work, particularly for teams building new analytical capabilities rather than maintaining existing pipelines.

The Core Ideas

Appetite, not estimates. Instead of asking "how long will this take?", Shape Up asks "how much time are we willing to spend on this?" The team sets an appetite — typically two or six weeks — and scopes the work to fit the appetite. If it can't be done in six weeks, the question is not "do we extend?" but "what is the most valuable subset we can deliver in six weeks?" This reframes the estimation problem entirely: you're not predicting complexity, you're setting a time budget.

Pitches instead of backlog grooming. Work enters a cycle not through a backlog but through a pitch — a document that describes the problem, the proposed solution at a sufficient level of detail, the appetite, and the risks. Pitches are prepared by a senior engineer or product manager, reviewed by a small group ("the betting table"), and either funded (added to the next cycle) or not. No pitch graveyard, no endless refinement of tickets that never ship.

Six-week cycles with two-week cooldown. The cycle is six weeks. At the end of the cycle, work ships or it doesn't — there are no extensions, no "let's just finish this one thing." The two-week cooldown that follows is unstructured time for engineering-led work: fixing the tech debt everyone knows about, exploring new approaches, writing documentation, or doing nothing in particular. The cooldown is intentional and protected.

Why It Fits Data Engineering

The appetite model handles the estimation problem that breaks Scrum: rather than trying to estimate how long a new data model will take, you ask "is this worth six weeks of engineering time?" Most data projects can be answered with that question. The cooldown maps well onto the data engineering reality that pipelines accumulate technical debt faster than they accumulate documented feature work — two weeks per quarter of protected tech-debt time is genuinely restorative.

Go DeeperEstimating data work — why ranges beat point estimates every time

Data engineering estimates are notoriously unreliable. Not because data engineers are poor estimators — because the uncertainty is irreducible at the time of estimation. A pipeline from a well-documented source to a well-understood destination might take two days. The same pipeline from a poorly-documented ERP system with no data dictionary might take two weeks. You often cannot know which you have until you're already in it.

Ranges communicate honesty: "This will take between three and ten days, depending on source data quality" is more accurate than "five days." It is also more useful to stakeholders, because it surfaces the uncertainty explicitly rather than creating false confidence that will need to be managed later when the five-day estimate slips to eight.

The three-phase estimate: Break data engineering work into three phases with separate estimates. Phase 1: Exploration (understanding the problem, profiling the source, assessing complexity) — time-boxed to two or three days, always. Phase 2: Development (building the pipeline, model, or report) — estimated after Phase 1 when you have real information. Phase 3: Validation (stakeholder sign-off, reconciliation, testing in production) — always treated as a separate item that depends on stakeholder availability.

The cost of not doing this: Teams that provide point estimates and then miss them damage their credibility with stakeholders faster than any technical failure. Stakeholders who receive honest ranges with clear uncertainty sources are far more forgiving of the upper end of the range than of a missed point estimate. Calibrating stakeholder expectations early is a senior practitioner skill, not a weakness.

🏢Field Note
A Financial Services Firm  ·  Kanban WIP Limits Prevent Burnout

A data engineering team of five at a financial services firm was running perpetually behind. Each engineer had between four and seven active work items at any given time — all nominally "in progress," all demanding attention. Morning stand-ups were consumed by status updates on things that hadn't moved. Retrospectives produced the same action items every cycle: "communicate blockers earlier," "reduce context switching."

The team migrated to Kanban with a WIP limit of two per engineer in the Development column. The first week was uncomfortable: engineers who had twelve things started found they were not allowed to start new work until existing work cleared the board. Some pushed back — "my items are blocked, I have to work on something." The Kanban response: work to unblock the blocked items first (escalate, find workarounds, communicate). If genuinely unable to move, it's a team problem, not an individual problem.

By week three, the average time an item spent in Development had halved. Context switching dropped. The blocked swimlane was now carrying only three items (down from eight), each with a clear owner and a visible timer. Senior stakeholders began seeing consistent throughput for the first time. The team was not working harder — they were working with less context switching per engineer per day.

The Lesson

WIP limits feel counterintuitive when you first apply them — "I have things to do, why can't I start them?" The answer: because starting more work when existing work is stuck doesn't move the stuck work. It adds cognitive load on top of a bottleneck. Finish before you start.

Go DeeperJIRA vs Azure Boards vs Linear vs Notion — choosing the right tracking tool

JIRA (Atlassian): The enterprise standard for Scrum and Kanban tracking. Highly configurable, integrates with Confluence for documentation, connects natively to Bitbucket and GitHub. The downside: overwhelming for small teams, slow UI, and a configuration surface area that invites over-engineering your process. Best for teams in organizations where JIRA is already the standard and migration isn't an option.

Azure Boards (Microsoft): The native work tracking tool within Azure DevOps. Excellent integration with Azure Repos, Azure Pipelines, and the rest of the Microsoft engineering ecosystem. If your team's source control is Azure DevOps, Azure Boards is the lowest-friction choice — work items, commits, and pipelines all linked natively. Less flexible than JIRA but sufficient for most data team workflows.

Linear: A modern, fast, opinionated project management tool popular with engineering teams. Excellent UX, cycles (analogous to sprints) and projects built-in, GitHub integration, and a command-palette-driven interface that experienced engineers find significantly faster than JIRA. Best for smaller, engineering-led teams with autonomy over their tooling. Not common in enterprise data contexts yet but growing.

Notion: Not a project management tool by design, but used as one by many data teams because documentation and task tracking live in the same place. Excellent for hybrid documentation-and-tracking workflows, Shape Up pitches, and knowledge bases. Poor for high-volume sprint tracking or anything requiring strong workflow automation. Best as a supplement to a proper tracking tool, not a replacement.

Recommendation for most data engineering teams: If inside Microsoft/Azure DevOps → Azure Boards. If in a Scrum org with existing JIRA → JIRA. If you have a choice → Linear for engineering-first experience, Notion alongside it for documentation and context.

✓ Section Summary

Delivery methodology matters because senior data engineering is a delivery leadership role. Standard Scrum breaks for data work in three structural ways: velocity cannot be predicted for exploratory work, Definition of Done is not binary when data quality is a spectrum, and external dependencies block work in ways the team cannot control. Kanban addresses all three: continuous flow, explicit blocked swimlane, WIP limits that prevent context-switching overload. Adapted Scrum (rough sizing, sprint buffer, explicit blocked state) is workable for teams inside Scrum organizations. Shape Up — appetite-based six-week cycles with two-week cooldowns — is an excellent fit for data engineering's combination of new capability building and accumulated technical debt. Regardless of methodology: write explicit Definitions of Done, estimate in ranges rather than points, and treat blocked items as visible team problems rather than individual failures. Tools: Azure Boards for Microsoft-stack teams, JIRA when mandated, Linear for engineering-first teams with autonomy.

Question 1 of 5

A data engineering team is running two-week sprints. In three consecutive sprints, their velocity has been 55%, 48%, and 52% of planned capacity. The retrospective consistently cites "blocked items" as the cause. What is the most likely structural explanation, and what is the correct response?

A
The engineers are under-performing; increase the sprint cadence to daily check-ins to identify blockers earlier
B
The backlog is poorly refined; spend more time on sprint planning to improve estimates
C
Data work is structurally dependent on external parties (IT, upstream system owners, business validators) that Scrum has no mechanism to manage; the correct response is to add a sprint buffer, make blocked items explicitly visible, and consider Kanban for high-dependency work types
D
Sprints are too short; switch to four-week sprints so there is more time to resolve blockers

C is correct. Consistently low velocity caused by blockers is a structural problem, not an individual performance problem or a planning problem. Data engineering is inherently dependent on external parties for access, validation, and upstream data quality — these are not resolvable by better retrospective action items. The appropriate adaptations are: (1) build a 15–20% sprint buffer for unplanned work, (2) add an explicit blocked swimlane with timestamps so stakeholders can see where work is stuck and why, (3) consider Kanban where blocked items can sit in a visible state without failing a sprint and parallel work continues. Longer sprints (D) do not resolve blocked items — they just make the failure take longer to become visible.

What is the purpose of a WIP (Work in Progress) limit in a Kanban board, and what happens when a column reaches its WIP limit?

A
WIP limits define the maximum number of stories allowed in a sprint; when reached, the sprint is closed early
B
WIP limits cap the number of items in a given column simultaneously; when reached, no new item can enter that column until an existing item exits — forcing the team to finish work before starting new work, exposing bottlenecks
C
WIP limits are advisory guidelines that signal the team should consider splitting large tickets
D
WIP limits apply only to the backlog and prevent it growing beyond a manageable size

B is correct. WIP limits are the mechanism that makes Kanban function. Without them, Kanban is just a list. When a column (say, Development: WIP 3) is full, no new work item can enter it — engineers must focus on moving existing work forward (clearing blockers, completing review, shipping). This prevents the common failure mode of many items "in progress" simultaneously where nothing actually finishes. WIP limits also surface bottlenecks structurally: if the Review column is perpetually at its limit, the bottleneck is review capacity, not development — a fact that would be invisible without WIP limits.

Question 3 of 5

In Shape Up methodology, what is an "appetite" and how does it differ from a traditional time estimate?

A
An appetite is an estimate expressed in story points rather than hours or days
B
An appetite is the number of engineers assigned to a piece of work for the cycle
C
An appetite is a time budget — how much time the team is willing to invest — set before scoping begins. The work is then scoped to fit the appetite, rather than the time being estimated from the scope
D
An appetite is a post-cycle review of how long work actually took, used to improve future estimates

C is correct. The appetite model inverts the estimation relationship. In traditional estimation: scope is fixed, estimate how long it takes. In Shape Up: time box is fixed (two weeks or six weeks), scope is negotiated to fit. "What is the most valuable version of this we can deliver in six weeks?" is the question. This sidesteps the irreducible uncertainty of data engineering estimation — you're no longer trying to predict complexity you can't yet see, you're setting a time investment limit and scoping accordingly. If the work can't be done in the appetite, the question isn't "do we extend?" but "what is the minimum valuable version?"

Question 4 of 5

A data pipeline has been built, passes all dbt tests, and is deployed to production. The engineer marks it "Done" in the sprint board. The following week, the Finance team runs their month-end report on it and discovers the revenue figures are 12% lower than expected due to a currency conversion rule the engineer wasn't aware of. What does this indicate about the team's Definition of Done?

A
The Definition of Done was correct — the engineer could not have known about the currency rule without being told; this is a requirements gap
B
The dbt tests should have caught this; the test coverage definition needs improving
C
The Definition of Done did not include business stakeholder validation; "technically passing" and "business-correct" are not the same state for data work, and the DoD must require explicit sign-off from the data consumer before closure
D
The sprint should have included the Finance team's validation as a separate ticket

C is correct. This is the core data engineering DoD problem. A pipeline that passes technical tests is technically done. A pipeline that accurately answers the business question it was built for is business-done. For financial or analytical data, those are not the same thing. A proper DoD for a revenue pipeline includes: data profiled, tests passing, and business stakeholder has validated sample output against a known correct figure. Moving the validation step into the DoD — not after it — is what prevents this failure mode. Option D is not wrong as a process suggestion but doesn't address the fundamental DoD gap: validation should be part of Done, not a separate ticket that can be deprioritized.

A
Azure Boards — it is native to Azure DevOps, linking work items directly to commits, pull requests, and pipeline runs in the same platform without any configuration

Scrum's Three Breaks

Velocity requires predictable work. DoD requires binary completeness. Scrum assumes internal blockers. Data engineering violates all three. Know the structural reasons, not just the symptoms.

Finish Before Starting

WIP limits enforce the most important Kanban principle. When a column is full, don't start new work — clear the bottleneck. Teams that finish things outperform teams that start things.

DoD Must Include Validation

"Technically passing" ≠ "business correct." Every data deliverable's Definition of Done must require stakeholder sign-off on sample output before the ticket closes.

Interview Insight

"How do you run a data engineering sprint?" Answer: describe what you keep from Scrum (cadence, retros, demo), what you modify (rough sizing, buffer, blocked state), and why. Mention Kanban as an alternative you know. Mentioning Shape Up at a senior level signals breadth.
