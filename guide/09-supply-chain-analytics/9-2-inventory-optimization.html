Inventory Optimization

9.2.1

The Core Tradeoff

Every inventory decision balances two costs pulling in opposite directions. Holding cost is what it costs to keep inventory on hand — warehouse space, capital tied up in unsold stock, insurance, spoilage or obsolescence risk. Stockout cost is what it costs when you don't have inventory when demand arrives — lost sales, expedited emergency shipping, and damaged customer trust that can outlast the immediate lost sale. Order too much and holding cost eats your margin; order too little and stockout cost does the same from the other direction.

There is no inventory strategy that minimizes both costs simultaneously — every method covered in this section is really a different answer to the same question: given genuine uncertainty about future demand (the forecasting problem from section 9.1), where is the point that minimizes the combined cost of holding too much and risking too little?
9.2.2

EOQ — Economic Order Quantity

EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) calculates the order quantity that minimizes the combined total of two specific costs: ordering cost (a roughly fixed cost incurred each time an order is placed, regardless of size — processing, shipping setup, supplier coordination) and holding cost per unit. Order in small, frequent batches and ordering cost adds up; order in large, infrequent batches and holding cost adds up. EOQ is the specific order size sitting at the bottom of that combined cost curve.

Figure 9.3As order quantity increases, ordering cost per unit falls (fewer orders needed) while holding cost rises (more inventory sitting in the warehouse at any given time). EOQ is the quantity at the minimum point of their combined total.
+
Go DeeperThe EOQ formula and what each assumption actually costs you if it's wrong

The classic EOQ formula is EOQ = √(2DS/H), where D is annual demand, S is the fixed cost per order, and H is the annual holding cost per unit. The formula is derived by writing total annual cost as a function of order quantity Q — (D/Q)×S for ordering cost plus (Q/2)×H for average holding cost — and finding the value of Q where that combined function is at its minimum, which calculus shows is exactly where the two cost curves cross, matching Figure 9.3.

The formula's elegance comes with real assumptions worth naming explicitly, because each one is a place the formula can mislead you if violated: demand is assumed constant and known (exactly the forecasting problem from section 9.1 — EOQ on its own says nothing about demand uncertainty, which is precisely why section 9.2.3's safety stock exists as a separate concept layered on top); ordering cost and holding cost per unit are assumed fixed regardless of order size (real suppliers often offer volume discounts that change this); and lead time (the delay between placing an order and receiving it) is assumed to be zero or perfectly predictable.

The practical takeaway: EOQ answers "how much should I order at a time, assuming I know demand exactly and lead times never vary?" — a genuinely useful baseline calculation, but one that explicitly does not address the question of demand uncertainty, which is exactly what the next section covers.

9.2.3

Safety Stock and Reorder Points

Safety stock is extra inventory held specifically to buffer against demand and lead-time uncertainty — the gap EOQ's clean assumptions deliberately leave unaddressed. The reorder point is the inventory level that triggers placing a new order: expected demand during the lead time, plus the safety stock buffer.

The critical connection back to section 9.1: safety stock should scale with forecast uncertainty, not be set as a flat number applied uniformly across every product. A SKU with highly stable, easily forecastable demand needs comparatively little safety stock buffer, because the forecast itself is rarely far off. A SKU with volatile, hard-to-forecast demand — exactly the kind of pattern that produces high forecast error in section 9.1's terms — needs substantially more safety stock to protect against the same probability of stocking out, even if its average demand level is identical to the stable SKU.

+
Go DeeperSizing safety stock from forecast error and a target service level

A common formula sizes safety stock as: Safety Stock = Z × σ × √(Lead Time), where σ (sigma) is the standard deviation of forecast error over a given period — the same kind of error measured by the MAE or MAPE metrics from section 9.1 — and Z is a value chosen from the standard normal distribution corresponding to a target service level (the probability of not stocking out before the next delivery arrives). A 95% service level uses a larger Z value (roughly 1.65) than an 85% service level (roughly 1.04), directly trading more safety stock for a lower stockout probability.

The formula makes the connection back to section 9.1 explicit and quantitative, not just conceptual: σ is computed directly from each SKU's own historical forecast errors, so a SKU with consistently small forecast misses (low σ) needs far less safety stock at the same service level than one with large, erratic misses (high σ) — precisely the distinction the distribution company field note learned the expensive way. The √(Lead Time) term adds a further, often underappreciated detail: uncertainty compounds over a longer lead time, so a supplier with a longer or more variable lead time needs proportionally more safety stock even at identical demand variability.

The business-facing translation of all this: a service level isn't a free choice with no cost attached — pushing from 90% to 99% service level on a volatile SKU can require a disproportionately large safety stock increase, since Z grows non-linearly as the target service level approaches 100%. Choosing a service level is itself a holding-cost-versus-stockout-cost tradeoff, the same core tension this section opened with, just expressed as a single tunable parameter.

📊Field Note
A Distribution Company  ·  The Flat Safety-Stock Rule That Cost Money in Both Directions

A distribution company applied a uniform policy — two weeks of average demand as safety stock — across its entire product catalog, regardless of how forecastable any individual SKU actually was. Stable, predictable products (steady year-round demand, low forecast error) were carrying far more safety stock than their actual uncertainty justified, tying up working capital and warehouse space for no real risk reduction. Simultaneously, a smaller set of highly volatile, hard-to-forecast products — new product launches, items with erratic seasonal spikes — were stocking out regularly under the exact same two-week rule, because two weeks of buffer was nowhere near enough to cover their much larger forecast uncertainty.

The fix, once the team connected forecast error (computed the same way as section 9.1's evaluation discussion) to safety-stock sizing directly, was to size each SKU's safety stock as a function of its own individual demand variability rather than a flat company-wide rule — reducing buffer on the stable products (freeing working capital) while increasing it specifically on the volatile ones (cutting their stockout rate), using the exact same total inventory budget as before, just allocated according to where uncertainty actually was.

The Lesson

Safety stock sized as a flat rule is both wasteful and risky at the same time, just on different SKUs. Sizing it from each product's actual forecast uncertainty — the direct output of section 9.1's forecasting work — is what makes inventory decisions and forecasting genuinely the same discipline rather than two separate problems handled by two separate teams.

9.2.4

Just-in-Time vs. Safety-Stock Strategies

Just-in-Time (JIT) inventory strategy minimizes held inventory by timing deliveries to arrive as close as possible to the moment of actual need, relying on highly reliable, often tightly coordinated suppliers rather than a large on-hand buffer. It minimizes holding cost aggressively — in stable, predictable-demand, reliable-supplier conditions it can be genuinely the lower-cost strategy — but it does so by removing exactly the buffer that safety stock exists to provide, which means it has comparatively little resilience against a sudden supply disruption or an unexpected demand spike.

Figure 9.4JIT and safety-stock strategies sit at opposite ends of a cost-vs-resilience tradeoff. Most real supply chains land somewhere between the two extremes, applying each strategy selectively by SKU risk profile rather than picking one universally.
+
Go DeeperWhy JIT's efficiency and its fragility are the same property

It's tempting to treat JIT's cost efficiency and its disruption fragility as two separate facts about the strategy. They're actually the same fact viewed from two angles: JIT achieves its lower holding cost specifically by removing the buffer that would otherwise absorb a supply delay or a demand spike. There is no version of JIT that keeps the cost savings but adds back meaningful resilience — the resilience and the buffer that provides it are the same inventory, and a strategy that minimizes one necessarily minimizes the other.

This is why the real-world decision is rarely "JIT or safety stock" applied uniformly across an entire catalog, but a selective application of each based on per-SKU risk: a single-source, long-lead-time, high-disruption-consequence component is a poor JIT candidate even within a company that runs JIT successfully elsewhere; a low-consequence, multi-sourced, short-lead-time commodity input is a much safer one. The strategic skill is in the segmentation, not in picking one philosophy company-wide.

⚙️Field Note
An Electronics Assembler  ·  The Single-Supplier Component That Stopped a Production Line

An electronics assembler ran a Just-in-Time strategy successfully across most of its component catalog — reliable suppliers, stable demand, meaningfully lower holding costs than competitors running larger buffers. One specific component, sourced from a single overseas supplier with a multi-week lead time, was managed under the exact same JIT policy as everything else, on the reasoning that the policy had worked well company-wide.

A disruption at that single supplier — unrelated to the assembler's own operations entirely — halted shipments for several weeks. With essentially no safety stock buffer for that specific component, under the company-wide JIT policy, production of every product using it stopped within days, while components managed with appropriate safety stock for their own risk profile were unaffected. The post-incident review's conclusion was specifically about segmentation: the company's JIT strategy itself wasn't wrong, but applying it uniformly to a single-source, long-lead-time, hard-to-substitute component was a risk-profile mismatch that a deliberate component-level risk assessment would have caught.

The Lesson

JIT's resilience tradeoff (covered in this section's deep dive) means a single inventory policy applied uniformly across an entire catalog is rarely correct. The components most vulnerable to a JIT policy are exactly the ones with single-source, long-lead-time, hard-to-substitute supply — precisely where a safety-stock buffer is needed most, even within an otherwise successful JIT operation.

9.2.5

Where Data Engineering Fits

Pulling this Part together: every concept covered — EOQ, safety stock, reorder points, the JIT/safety-stock risk tradeoff — depends on a working data pipeline underneath it. EOQ needs accurate, current per-unit ordering and holding cost data. Safety stock sizing needs the forecast error metrics from section 9.1, computed continuously as new actuals arrive, not calculated once and left stale. Reorder points need real-time or near-real-time inventory level data flowing reliably from warehouse systems. None of this analytical layer functions without exactly the ingestion, transformation, and data quality work this entire guide has built toward — which is the precise sense in which supply chain analytics, like the classical ML and statistics covered in Part 8, is a domain a senior data engineer is expected to support fluently, even without owning the inventory policy decisions themselves.
